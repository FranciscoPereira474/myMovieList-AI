A Terrifying Look in the Mirror

Contains Spoilers

This review discusses the ending. Proceed with caution.

Alex Garland has never been interested in comforting his audience. From the claustrophobic dread of Ex Machina to the biological mutations of Annihilation, he forces us to look at things we’d rather ignore. With Civil War, he strips away the sci-fi metaphor and points the camera directly at modern polarization.

The sound design deserves an Oscar immediately. The gunshots aren't cinematic "pew-pews"; they are deafening, terrifying cracks of thunder that shake the theater seats. It anchors the film in a reality that feels uncomfortably close to home.

"Every time I survived a war zone, I thought I was sending a warning home: don’t do this. But here we are."

Kirsten Dunst gives a career-best performance as Lee. Her weariness is palpable. She moves through the chaos not like a hero, but like a ghost haunting her own life. The dynamic between her and the rookie photographer, Jessie (Cailee Spaeny), forms the emotional spine of the movie.

Hover to reveal spoiler

The ending sequence in the White House is pure nightmare fuel. When they finally reach the President, the anti-climax of his capture—dragged out from under a desk like a coward—is brilliant. It refuses to mythologize power. It just shows it ending, brutally and abruptly.

Ultimately, this isn't a movie about politics. It's a movie about war journalists, and the cost of being a witness. It asks: does taking the picture change anything? Or are we just documenting our own demise?
